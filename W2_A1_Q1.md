# Question 1: Grain Definition & Column Classification

## 🎯 Objective
Before writing any DDL to build the data warehouse, the fundamental first step is defining the architectural blueprint. This document outlines the conceptual design for the `FactTicketSales` table by establishing its exact grain and classifying the raw source columns into dimensions and measures. Establishing this upfront prevents ambiguous joins and double-counting in downstream reporting.

## 🌾 The Grain Statement
The grain dictates exactly what a single row in the fact table represents. 

**Grain:** One row represents a single confirmed ticket (booking) sold for a specific flight.

## 🗂️ Column Classification
Evaluating the candidate columns from the raw source tables (`bronze_bookings` joined to its respective `bronze_flights` record) to determine their role in the dimensional model.

### Dimension Keys (The Slicing Axes - Who, What, Where, When)
These source columns provide the context for our business metrics. In the final physical model, these will guide the creation of integer-based Surrogate Keys to optimize engine performance and handle historical changes.
* `booking_date` *(from bronze_bookings)*
* `travel_date` *(from bronze_bookings)*
* `passenger_id` *(from bronze_bookings)*
* `flight_id` *(from bronze_bookings)*
* `origin_airport_code` *(from bronze_flights)*
* `dest_airport_code` *(from bronze_flights)*
* `aircraft_code` *(from bronze_flights)*
* `fare_class` *(from bronze_bookings - Categorical attribute)*
* `booking_id` *(from bronze_bookings - Degenerate dimension)*

### Measures (The Numbers)
These are the quantitative metrics that the business wants to aggregate and analyze.
* `fare_amount` *(from bronze_bookings)*
* `tax_amount` *(from bronze_bookings)*
* `miles_earned` *(from bronze_bookings)*

## ➕ Additive vs. Non-Additive Metrics
Understanding how measures aggregate is critical for building a reliable semantic layer for the analytics team.

* **Additive Measures:** The `fare_amount`, `tax_amount`, and `miles_earned` are fully additive. This means they can be safely and accurately summed across any dimension (e.g., calculating the total revenue generated out of a specific airport, or total miles earned by a passenger over a year).
* **Non-Additive Values:** A column like `booking_id` is strictly non-additive. Even though it is stored as a number, summing transactional IDs together yields a mathematically meaningless result. It functions purely as an identifier for the transaction.
